Wooden Lid Reference (what we're replacing)

The Weck acacia wood lid for the large size is ~110mm outer diameter, about 8-10mm thick, and ~40g. It has a silicone gasket ring on the underside that seats inside the rim. It simply rests on top — no threading, no clamp, just gravity and a slight friction fit from the gasket.

Design target: Our smart lid should match ~110mm outer diameter, stay under 20mm thick, and under 80g. It drops onto the same rim in the same way. Anyone picking it up shouldn't think "gadget" — they should think "nice lid."

3. Component Walkthrough

Here's every component inside the lid, what it does, and exactly how it connects. Think of the lid as a sandwich: top shell → PCB → battery → bottom shell.

⚡
ESP32-C3 Super Mini
The brain — WiFi, BLE, and GPIO in a tiny package

This is the microcontroller that runs everything. The ESP32-C3 is a single-core RISC-V chip with built-in WiFi (2.4GHz 802.11 b/g/n) and Bluetooth Low Energy 5.0. The "Super Mini" dev board is only 22.5 × 18mm — small enough to fit inside the lid with room to spare. It runs the firmware, reads all sensors, updates the e-ink display, and sends data over WiFi to your phone/app.

Why the C3 over the original ESP32? Much lower deep-sleep current (~5µA vs ~10µA on the S3), smaller physical board, and it has enough GPIO pins for our needs. We don't need the dual-core power of the S3 since we're doing simple sensor reads and WiFi bursts.

VL53L0X Time-of-Flight Distance Sensor
Laser distance measurement — the core measuring instrument

This tiny laser sensor measures the distance from the lid down to the starter surface. It emits an invisible infrared laser pulse and measures how long it takes to bounce back — hence "time of flight." It has a 25° cone of detection, which at 100mm distance covers about a 44mm circle — more than enough to reliably detect the starter surface even if it's bubbly and uneven.

How it tracks the rise: Right after you feed and replace the lid, the firmware takes a baseline reading — say 120mm to the starter surface. As the starter rises, that distance shrinks. When it hits ~60mm, the starter has doubled. The firmware tracks the rate of change: when the distance stops decreasing and starts increasing, the starter has peaked and is falling back. That's your alert.

Mounting: The sensor module (4.4 × 2.4 × 1.0mm on a small breakout board ~13 × 18mm) mounts to the bottom shell, pointing straight down through a 12mm window. The window needs to be open (not covered with plastic) since even clear plastic can scatter the IR laser. A small recessed lip around the opening keeps flour dust from falling directly onto the sensor lens.

BME280 Environmental Sensor
Temperature, humidity, and barometric pressure

Temperature is arguably more predictive than height for estimating when your starter will peak. At 72°F (22°C) it might take 8-12 hours. At 80°F (27°C) it could peak in 4-6 hours. The BME280 gives you all three environmental factors: temperature, relative humidity, and barometric pressure. Over time, with enough logged data, the AI model can correlate all three with rise speed to make increasingly accurate predictions.

Mounting: On the bottom shell, offset from center, with small vent holes (1.5mm diameter × 4-5 holes) in the shell beneath it. This lets it read the air directly above the starter surface inside the jar — which is the most relevant temperature reading. The vent holes are too small for starter to reach through even on a vigorous rise.

1.54" E-Ink Display (200×200, B/W)
Glanceable info without pulling out your phone

The e-ink display sits in a recessed window on the top surface of the lid. It shows three key numbers: rise percentage (how much the starter has risen since feeding), current temperature, and estimated time to peak. Because e-ink holds its image with zero power draw once written, this is extremely battery-friendly — it only draws current during the brief refresh (~2 seconds every 15 minutes).

Why e-ink and not OLED? An OLED draws power continuously while displaying. E-ink draws power only when changing. Since our display updates once every 15 minutes, e-ink uses roughly 1/500th the energy. Plus it's easily readable in any lighting — including the bright kitchen lights above your counter.

Display layout: The 200×200 pixel canvas is divided into three zones. Left: large rise percentage with a small arrow icon. Center: mini bar graph showing the rise curve. Right: temperature and time estimate. A small icon shows WiFi status.

LiPo Battery + TP4056 Charge Controller
Rechargeable power — 7-10 days per charge

A flat lithium polymer cell sits beneath the PCB. At 500mAh, it provides enough juice for 7-10 days of operation at 15-minute measurement intervals. The TP4056 charge controller manages USB-C charging safely — it handles the constant-current/constant-voltage profile that LiPo cells need and cuts off at full charge. An onboard low-battery LED or e-ink battery icon warns you when it's time to charge.

Charging workflow: Pull the lid off the jar, plug in a USB-C cable, set it on the counter. Takes about 1.5 hours from empty. You could charge overnight, or even use the jar with the cable plugged in — it's just a lid, the cable doesn't interfere with anything.

4. How Everything Connects

The ESP32-C3 is the hub. The two sensors (VL53L0X and BME280) share the same I²C bus — that's just two wires (SDA + SCL) for both sensors. The e-ink display uses SPI — four wires (CLK, MOSI, CS, DC) plus a busy pin and reset. The LED is a single GPIO. Total wiring is compact and manageable.

ComponentInterfaceESP32-C3 PinsNotes
VL53L0X (ToF) I²C GPIO4 SDA, GPIO5 SCL Address 0x29, shared bus with BME280
BME280 (env) I²C GPIO4 SDA, GPIO5 SCL Address 0x76, same bus — no conflict
E-Ink Display SPI GPIO6 CLK, GPIO7 MOSI, GPIO10 CS, GPIO3 DC, GPIO2 BUSY, GPIO1 RST Waveshare 1.54" B/W v2 module
RGB LED GPIO GPIO8 WS2812B single-pixel (data only)
Battery voltage ADC GPIO0 (ADC1_CH0) Voltage divider to read LiPo level
USB-C USB GPIO18 D-, GPIO19 D+ Native USB on C3, charge via TP4056

I²C pull-ups: Both sensors need 4.7kΩ pull-up resistors on SDA and SCL. Just two resistors, soldered on the PCB between the bus lines and 3.3V. Without them, communication will be flaky.

6. Power Budget

The lid spends 99.7% of its time in deep sleep. Every 15 minutes it wakes for a "measurement cycle" that lasts about 5-8 seconds: read sensors, compute, update display, send WiFi data, go back to sleep.

Battery life estimate: 500mAh ÷ 15.1mAh/day ≈ 33 days theoretical. With real-world inefficiencies (LDO dropout, WiFi retries, temperature effects), expect 10-20 days comfortably. Even the conservative end means you're charging twice a month.

Power optimization tricks for later: Skip WiFi on every cycle and batch-send every 4th cycle (saves ~75% of WiFi energy). Use BLE instead of WiFi when the phone is nearby. Only do a full e-ink refresh every 4th cycle and partial refresh in between. These tweaks could push real-world battery life to 3-4 weeks.

7. WiFi + App — Absolutely v1

Why WiFi Belongs in v1

The ESP32-C3 already has WiFi — it's not an add-on, it's built into the chip. Not using it would be like buying a car and not using the stereo. The e-ink display shows you what's happening now. WiFi is what lets the system notify you when you're not looking, which is the whole point — you want to go about your day and get a ping when the starter peaks.

What WiFi Enables in v1

01
Push notifications — "Your starter peaked at 2:34pm — time to mix your dough!" Sent via a simple webhook to Pushover, Ntfy, or your own server. This is the killer feature.
↓
02
Data logging — Every measurement (distance, temp, humidity, timestamp) is sent to a simple API or local database. This builds the dataset for AI predictions later.
↓
03
Live dashboard — A web app (or phone app) showing the real-time rise curve, current readings, and historical bakes. You could check it from work.
↓
04
OTA firmware updates — Over-the-air updates so you can push new features and bug fixes to the lid without plugging in USB. The ESP32 supports this natively.
↓
05
Home Assistant / MQTT integration — If you're into smart home stuff, the lid publishes to MQTT topics. Home Assistant can automate around it — turn on the oven at the right time, trigger a smart plug, etc.

WiFi Architecture Options

Simplest (v1 recommended): The ESP32 makes an HTTP POST to a lightweight cloud function or your own server every cycle. The server stores data and triggers push notifications. The phone app is a simple web app that reads from that API. No complex infrastructure needed — a free-tier Firebase, Supabase, or even a Raspberry Pi on your home network works.

Local-only option: If you don't want cloud, the ESP32 runs a tiny web server on your local network. You access the dashboard by visiting its IP address on your phone's browser. Push notifications go through a local MQTT broker. Everything stays on your WiFi, nothing leaves your home.

BLE fallback: If WiFi setup is too fiddly for some users, the ESP32-C3 also has BLE. The phone app could connect directly via Bluetooth when in range — great for initial setup, configuration, and real-time monitoring when you're in the kitchen. WiFi handles the "alert me when I'm not looking" use case.

8. The Measurement Cycle — Step by Step

Here's exactly what happens every 15 minutes inside the lid:

01
Wake from deep sleep — RTC timer fires after 15 min. ESP32-C3 boots (~150ms). Initializes I²C bus and SPI bus.
↓
02
Read VL53L0X — Takes a single distance measurement (~33ms). Returns distance in mm. If reading is >500mm or <20mm, flag as "lid removed" or "starter touching sensor" and handle accordingly.
↓
03
Read BME280 — Forced read of temperature, humidity, pressure (~50ms). Stores in memory.
↓
04
Compute rise state — Compare current distance to baseline (stored in RTC memory between sleeps). Calculate rise %. Determine if we're in the rising, peaked, or falling phase by comparing to last 3-4 readings. Estimate time to peak based on current rate and temperature.
↓
05
Update e-ink display — Write rise %, temp, and time estimate to the screen (~2 sec refresh). On every 4th cycle, do a full refresh to prevent ghosting; otherwise partial refresh.
↓
06
Connect WiFi + send data — Connect to saved network (~1-2 sec). POST JSON payload: {distance, temp, humidity, pressure, rise_pct, state, battery_v, timestamp}. If peak detected, trigger push notification. Disconnect WiFi.
↓
07
Pulse LED — Quick color pulse (200ms) to indicate current state. Green = rising. Amber = near peak. Red = peaked.
↓
08
Enter deep sleep — Store critical state (baseline distance, cycle count, peak status) in RTC memory (persists through deep sleep). Set timer for 15 min. Power down everything. Total cycle: ~5-8 seconds.

Special Events

Lid removed (distance > 500mm): The ToF sensor reads a huge distance or times out. The firmware logs "lid removed" and enters a waiting state — it keeps checking every 30 seconds in low-power mode. When the distance returns to a plausible range, it assumes you've fed the starter and replaced the lid. It takes a new baseline reading and starts a fresh tracking cycle. The e-ink updates to show "New cycle started."

Starter touching sensor (distance < 20mm): Extremely vigorous rise. Log a warning, send an alert ("your starter is overflowing!"), and increase measurement frequency to every 5 minutes to catch the peak precisely.
